Civil judicial

Civil claims, in both Islamic jurisprudence (Shariah law) and international legal terminology, refer to disputes concerning personal rights, financial matters, property ownership, and other non-criminal issues. These claims primarily deal with the enforcement and protection of private rights rather than penal sanctions.

In Islamic (Shariah) Law

  • Civil claims (دعوې مدني) encompass disputes related to ownership (ملکیت), tenancy (سرای) housing (کور), and land ownership (ځمکې ملکیت). These claims are adjudicated based on principles derived from the Quran, Hadith, and Islamic legal scholarship.
  • Property rights are protected under Shariah, emphasizing justice, fairness, and the fulfillment of contractual obligations.
  • The resolution of such claims involves mediation, arbitration, or court judgments that align with Islamic ethical and legal standards.
  • Financial disputes and personal rights claims are also addressed within this framework, ensuring that parties receive their due rights without violating Islamic prohibitions such as usury (riba)

In International Legal Terminology

  • Civil claims refer to legal actions brought to resolve disputes between private parties over rights and obligations, excluding criminal offenses.
  • These include claims related to ownership of movable and immovable property, contractual disagreements, torts, and family law matters.
  • The legal framework governing these claims is based on codified civil laws, international treaties, and conventions where applicable.
  • International law recognizes the protection of property rights and personal claims through mechanisms like civil litigation, arbitration, and alternative dispute resolution.

Summary

Civil claims in both Shariah and international law cover disputes involving personal and financial rights, especially concerning property such as houses, land, and tenancy rights. They focus on resolving conflicts through legal processes that uphold ownership rights and contractual duties without involving criminal penalties. Types of Civil Disputes

1. Property Ownership, Houses, and Land Ownership Disputes:
  • In Islamic jurisprudence, ownership of property, including houses and land, is governed by principles derived from the Quran and the Sunnah, interpreted through schools of Islamic law such as Hanafi or Jafari. Ownership rights are respected and protected, but disputes often arise regarding rightful possession, inheritance, or transfer of property. Sharia mandates clear documentation (qabalah or deed) and lawful transfer procedures to establish ownership. Disputes are frequently resolved through a combination of formal courts applying Islamic law and informal community mechanisms such as elders or Jirga, which aim to reconcile parties in accordance with Sharia and customary law (urf), provided these do not contradict Islamic principles. The courts often supervise and record such settlements to ensure compliance with Sharia
2. Tenancy (Lease or Rental) Disputes:
  • Conflicts arising from tenancy agreements, including rent payment and property use, are addressed under Islamic contract law, which emphasizes fairness, mutual consent, and fulfillment of contractual obligations. The lessor and lessee have clearly defined rights and duties, and disputes are settled by courts or local dispute resolution bodies. Islamic law prohibits exploitation and unjust enrichment, thus protecting tenants from arbitrary eviction or excessive rent. Informal dispute resolution mechanisms also play a vital role in resolving such conflicts swiftly and amicably
3. Inheritance Division, Wills, and Succession:
  • Inheritance under Islamic law is a well-defined and detailed system derived from Quranic injunctions, particularly in Surah An-Nisa. The shares of heirs—such as children, spouses, parents, and siblings—are fixed and obligatory. Wills (wasiyyah) may allocate up to one-third of the estate to non-heirs or charitable causes, but cannot override the mandatory shares. Disputes over inheritance are common and often involve women’s rights, which Sharia explicitly protects, though customary practices may marginalize them. Formal courts and religious authorities enforce inheritance rights according to Islamic jurisprudence, often involving community elders to mediate. The Islamic Emirate of Afghanistan, for example, has institutional mechanisms like the Amr bil-Maruf to enforce Sharia in inheritance disputes, ensuring women receive their rightful shares despite social challenges
4. Claims for Compensation (Damages):
  • Claims for compensation in Islamic law arise when harm or loss (daman or ta'awun) is caused to a person or property. The principle of justice requires that the injured party be compensated fairly, restoring them to their prior position as much as possible. Compensation may be monetary or involve restitution. Islamic legal tradition provides detailed rules on liability and damages, emphasizing accountability and fairness. Dispute resolution often involves both formal courts and customary mechanisms, with religious scholars playing a role in interpreting applicable laws and ensuring just outcomes
5. Disputes over Deeds, Titles, and Transfers:
  • Disputes concerning the validity of deeds (qabalah), title documents, and property transfers are common in jurisdictions where formal land registration systems coexist with customary and Islamic legal traditions. Islamic law requires clear, consensual, and documented transfer of ownership to prevent disputes. Courts and local councils examine the authenticity of deeds, adherence to legal formalities, and the intent of parties. Informal dispute resolution bodies and religious authorities often assist in mediating conflicts, ensuring that transfers comply with Sharia and protect the rights of all parties involved. The integration of Islamic and customary law aims to provide accessible, culturally legitimate, and effective resolution
